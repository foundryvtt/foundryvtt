Foundry Virtual Tabletop was built with modularity in mind and provides a strong framework for creating and building Add-on Modules and Game Systems. Creating content often requires adhering to policies from outside the Foundry community.

Many publishers and game companies provide ways to license and build supplementary material for their games, and it can sometimes be difficult to understand what is allowed under those licenses. This article is intended to help shed some light on the development process and help you do your best to not run into problems with publishers.

Disclaimer: This article was not written under the guidance of any legal professionals and should not be construed as legal advice. It is only intended to suggest some best practices in licensing content.

This article was written based on a number of other articles as well as close reading of the licenses offered by various companies, source references to those can be found below.

Copyright, Trademark, and Licenses

To understand the difficulties related to licensing when creating content for a virtual tabletop, it is important to have at least a basic understanding of Copyright, Trademark, License Agreements, and how they all work together to protect Intellectual Property.

In general terms, Intellectual Property (IP) refers to anything that someone has created. The owner of a piece of Intellectual Property has rights to that property protected by law, most often, this means Copyright and Trademark law.

Copyright

Copyright law governs when it is acceptable to reproduce, publish, sell, or distribute Intellectual Property. When it comes to releasing fan-created content for a role-playing game, the very particular part of Copyright Law that concerns us is "Derivative Works". Any content which contains a part of, or are based upon, pre-existing Intellectual Property is considered a "Derivative Work".

The owner of a piece of Intellectual Property has the exclusive right to authorize creation of derivative works.

Trademark Trademark protections exist to make sure that Intellectual Property from one creator is not confused with the Intellectual Property of another. Trademarks are words, names, and symbols used to identify products.

Exclusive rights to a Trademark are awarded to the first to use it in commerce.

Licenses

Under the broader category of Intellectual Property law, a Licensing Agreement allows the owner of a piece of Intellectual Property to give permission to another party to use that IP. This can mean allowing someone to create a derivative work, or to create officially licensed products based on that IP.

There are a wide variety of license agreements from various publishers, many of which are available directly on the website of the company that publishes the material you want to use. A license agreement typically contains very specific legal terms outlining what is and isn't okay to use when creating fan content based on their IP. These licenses often require an application process for permission, or in some cases they are open for use provided the terms are followed.

The Open Game License

Perhaps the most prolific License Agreement in the RPG publishing industry, the Open Game License (OGL) was published by Wizards of the Coast in 2000, outlining rules for using their game content.

The OGL's language is verbose and legal. To understand it as best as we are able (we are not lawyers, after all) we will attempt to break it down into simple language. When creating content using the OGL, there are a few key areas that often go overlooked or misunderstood. For deeper understanding of the OGL, or further information on any portion of this breakdown, we suggest reading the OGL you intend to use very closely.

Permission

The OGL gives permission to use the data, usually contained in a System Reference Document (SRD) that the OGL comes packaged with or linked to. This data can be copied in whole or in part, as long as the requirements of the license are followed.

Copyright Notice and Copy of License

The OGL comes packaged with some legal boilerplate text which must be updated to match your content when you are distributing it, for every company you are using OGL content from. In addition, if any content you are distributing is based on the content released under the OGL, you must include a copy of the OGL with your content.

Product Identity

The OGL defines a list of terms which the Publisher considers as Product Identity. This list can be as vague or specific as the publisher cares to be, as these terms are not necessarily Trademarks.

The OGL forbids the use of those terms anywhere in relation to the content you are producing using the data from the SRD. Anywhere. This includes indicating the content you created is compatible, or intended for use with, the original game.

That means that you can't create a Game System or Add-on Module for Foundry VTT that contains words like D&D, Pathfinder, or a number of other titles in the name of that Game System or Module. Except if:

  • You have a specific, secondary license from the publisher which allows you to do so.
  • The publisher has a secondary Community or Fan license agreement or policy which allows you to do so.
  • You have contacted the publisher and received express written permission to do so.

The most common things considered Product Identity by a publisher are its list of Trademarks, but many publishers add additional words and logos that it considers proprietary to the game system to their list of Product Identity, which have not been trademarked.

So I can't even say my D&D 5E module containing new monsters is made for D&D?

That is correct. Fortunately, a number of publishers recognize the problem in that, and have released secondary policies that allow for use of Product Identity in very specific cases. Particularly ones which are not for commercial use. You will need to check if the publisher of the RPG you are making content has such a secondary policy. Wizards of the Coast, Paizo, Pelgrane Press, Monte Cook, and many other major publishers have such policies.

Be sure you understand the terms of community use and fan content policies before using them, and if you have any questions reach out to the publisher for clarification.

Basic Rules and the System Reference Document

Most publishers of RPGs release, in one way or another, very basic rules for players to get started with their games. In a lot of cases this takes the form of a System Reference Document (in the cases of OGL games) or a form of "quick-start" or "basic" rules document.

The content of those documents are usually safe to use and package in a Game System or Add-on Module for use with Foundry VTT. To be sure, it is best to check the first few pages of the document from the publisher to see if there are any restrictions on redistributing the information that the document contains.

Why are most systems for Foundry limited to SRD and OGL content?

Foundry does not at this time have a commercial marketplace or any commercial license with a publisher to allow their official content to be distributed. Game Systems are community-driven, and developed by dedicated community members who distribute their hard work for free to the rest of the community.

I do not live in the United States of America, does all of this really apply to me?

Very likely. Most countries work to ensure Intellectual Property receives equal protections under an agreement called the Berne Convention.
